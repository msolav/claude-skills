# CALQ jury rubrics — the actual score-band language

From CALQ's official *grilles d'évaluation* (EN 2025–26). These are the literal instruments jury members fill in: for each sub-criterion they pick one of **Excellent / Very good / Good / Fair / Poor**, and CALQ publishes the sentence that defines each band.

**This is the closest thing that exists to a marking scheme.** Read the band language, then write to clear the band you need.

---

## The two facts that reframe everything

**1. "Good" is not good.** Look at what "Good" actually says:

> *Artistic value of the project — **Good**: "The project is interesting and the applicant's intention is clearly defined."*

A clear, interesting, well-explained project earns the **middle band**. Clarity is the price of entry, not a differentiator. **Excellent** requires:

> *"The artistic proposal is remarkable. It is outstanding in form or content."*

Most artists write to clear the "Good" bar and are surprised to lose. The gap between *clearly defined* and *remarkable* is the whole competition.

**2. The scoring is explicitly comparative.** The "Poor" band repeatedly reads *"Relative to all the applications received…"* — the jury is not measuring you against a standard but against the pile in front of them. This is stated in the instrument itself.

## Rubric structure by component (visual arts)

All components: **Artistic evaluation = 100% of the score**, split into *Quality of artistic work* and *Interest and relevance of the project*. Administrative evaluation is a separate **Satisfactory / Non-satisfactory** gate.

### Creation — 30% / 70%
- **Quality of artistic work (30%):** quality of the applicant's past work
- **Interest and relevance (70%):**
  - Artistic value of the project
  - Relevance of the composition of the team (if applicable)
  - **Impact of the project on the evolution of the candidate's work**
  - **Impact and benefits of the project**

### Exploration and Research — 30% / 70%
- **Quality of artistic work (30%):** quality of past work
- **Interest and relevance (70%):**
  - Interest of the project to advance diverse artistic practices
  - Relevance of the team composition (if applicable)
  - Impact on the evolution of the candidate's work
  - **Innovative character of the project**
  - **Thoroughness and structure of the research process**

### Public Presentation — 30% / 70%
- **Quality of artistic work (30%):** quality of past work
- **Interest and relevance (70%):**
  - Artistic value of the project
  - Relevance of the team composition (if applicable)
  - Impact on the evolution of the applicant's work
  - **Scope of the planned dissemination** (attendance, number of presentations, territories)
  - **Suitability of the hosting structures**
- *Administrative adds three extra gates:* **diversity of revenue sources** · scope of the presenting partner's contribution · contribution of other collaborators

### Professional Development — 40% / 60%
- **Quality of artistic work (40%):** quality of past work
- **Interest and relevance (60%):**
  - Quality of the professional development program
  - **Choice of host structure, master or mentor**
  - Impact on the evolution of the applicant's work/career

## What "Excellent" requires, sub-criterion by sub-criterion

Paraphrasing the top band — this is what you must actually make true, not merely assert:

| Sub-criterion | "Good" (middle) | **"Excellent"** (top) |
|---|---|---|
| **Quality of past work** | "Generally interesting" | "Remarkable. The entire body of work is impressive." |
| **Artistic value of project** | "Interesting, intention clearly defined" | "Remarkable. **Outstanding in form or content.**" |
| **Team composition** | "Appropriate, should contribute positively" | "**Judicious**, brings an **exceptional contribution**" |
| **Impact on evolution of the work** | "Will have an impact" | "Will **surely** have a **major** impact" |
| **Impacts and benefits** | "Promises attractive benefits" | "Will **definitely** yield **major** benefits" |
| **Interest for advancing practices** *(Exploration)* | "Interest has been demonstrated" | "**Very persuasive**; interest to advance the practice is **outstanding**" |
| **Innovative character** *(Exploration)* | "Demonstrated; contributes to interesting avenues" | "**Clearly moves into unexplored territory** by presenting new avenues" |
| **Rigour and structure of research** *(Exploration)* | "Clearly demonstrated; approach is appropriate" | "**Impressive rigour.** The approach is **solid and cohesive**" |
| **Scope of dissemination** *(Public Presentation)* | "Good potential" | "**Excellent potential**" — evidenced by attendance, number of presentations, territories |
| **Suitability of hosting structures** *(Public Presentation)* | "Recognized and appropriate" | "**Recognized and ideally suited** to the nature of the work; an **undeniable asset**" |
| **Quality of PD program** *(Prof. Dev.)* | "Satisfactory and aligned with objectives" | "**Outstanding.** Approach and content aligned with the artist's objectives **in every respect**" |
| **Choice of mentor/host** *(Prof. Dev.)* | "Satisfactory and able to support" | "Stands out for its **reputation or high level of expertise**; **perfectly aligned** with objectives" |

### How to use this when drafting

The top-band language is dominated by three moves:

1. **Assertion of magnitude** — *major*, *outstanding*, *remarkable*. You cannot claim these; you have to *demonstrate* them. The jury writes "major impact" only if your own evidence forces the word.
2. **Named, evidenced specifics** — "recognized and ideally suited," "reputation or high level of expertise." This is why naming the venue, the mentor, the partner is not a nicety: two whole sub-criteria are *about* whether your chosen host/mentor is demonstrably excellent. Attach the evidence of their standing.
3. **Explicit demonstration** — the "Fair"/"Poor" bands are full of "not sufficiently demonstrated" / "not demonstrated" / "no justification is presented." **Applications lose points for things that were true but unstated.** If the project *will* have a major impact on your practice, the grid gives you no credit unless you say so, and say why.

**The single most repeated failure phrase across every grid is "not demonstrated."** Before submitting, go sub-criterion by sub-criterion and ask: *where, in the text, is this demonstrated?* If you can't point at a sentence, the jury can't either.

## Remuneration: paying the minimum is only "Satisfactory"

The French *Création* grid scores remuneration on the full five-point scale, and the bands are explicit:

| Band | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Excellent** | Remuneration is "in perfect adequation with the applicant's experience" — the project *distinguishes itself* on fairness of pay |
| **Very good** | Pay sits "**rightly above** the standards of collective agreements or minimum scales" |
| **Satisfactory** | "Acceptable. **It respects the established minimum standards.**" |
| **Fair (réserves)** | Not really matched to experience level; **no justification presented** |
| **Poor** | "Entirely unrealistic" |

**Paying yourself the CARFAC-RAAV minimum lands you at "Satisfactory" — the middle.** Paying *above* the minimum, and justifying it against your years of experience, is what reads as "Very good" or "Excellent." And note the recurring trigger: *"no justification is presented."* **Justify the rate in the budget notes**, don't just state it.

> ⚠️ **Version discrepancy, stated honestly:** the EN 2025–26 grids show administrative evaluation as a binary **Satisfactory / Non-satisfactory**, while the undated FR *Création* grid shows the five-point scale above. The EN 25–26 grids are the more recent and should govern. Either way the *substance* holds — minimum-standard pay is the floor, not the ceiling, and unjustified pay is penalized. Confirm the live scale with the program officer if it matters.

## Administrative gate — the recurring language

Whatever the scale, these are the tests:

- **Work plan:** "realistic and in line with the project objectives." Fails on "inconsistencies… (duration, missing elements)."
- **Budget:** "realistic and **sufficiently detailed for the type of project**." Fails on "some budget items are **not justified or accurately estimated**, there are missing items." The FR "Poor" band adds: *"there is an imbalance"* — i.e. a budget that doesn't balance.
- **Public Presentation only:** revenue sources must be **diversified**; the presenting partner's contribution must be substantial enough to "ensure good conditions."

## Coverage note

**Full band language (Excellent→Poor) verified** for: **Creation, Exploration and Research, Public Presentation, Professional Development** (EN grids 2025–26) + Création (FR).

**Weightings and sub-criteria verified, band language not yet obtained** for: **Travel, Circulation Within Quebec, Circulation Outside Quebec, Publication and Critical Documentation, Strategic Opportunities** — see `funders.md` for their criteria.

For those five, the *structure* is confirmed identical (100% artistic split two ways + an administrative gate), so the two headline lessons above almost certainly transfer: **"Good" means merely clear**, and **"not demonstrated" is the dominant failure mode**. But do not assume the sub-criteria mirror the four grids above — Public Presentation and Strategic Opportunities each carry sub-criteria and administrative gates the others don't. Ask the program officer for the grid; CALQ links one from every program page.
