# Writing craft: section by section

## Who you are actually writing for

A peer assessor: a working artist or curator, unpaid-ish, reading dozens of applications in a compressed window, scoring against a published grid, then defending a ranking in a committee meeting. They **want** to like the application. They are not looking for reasons to reject; they are looking for reasons to **rank**.

Three consequences:
1. **Give them the sentence they can repeat in the room.** Committee members advocate out loud for applications they remember. If a project cannot be summarized in one line by someone else, it will not be championed.
2. **Make scoring effortless.** If the criterion is "relevance of the project," a paragraph should visibly be *about* relevance. Do not make an assessor excavate.
3. **Comparative, not absolute.** Merit is ranked against the other applications in the cohort. Specificity is how an application separates.

---

## The summary line

Usually 25–50 words. Written **last**, from the finished application. It must contain: who, what form, what question, where, when. It is the sentence that gets repeated in committee.

Bad: *"An exploration of memory, identity and belonging through mixed media."*
Better: *"A six-month studio research period producing twelve large-scale cyanotypes made with river water from three sites along the Saint-Lawrence, culminating in a solo exhibition at [venue], March 2027."*

The second one is nameable, arguable, and fundable. The first one is 40,000 other applications.

---

## Project description — the spine

The jury needs, in roughly this order:

1. **What you will actually make or do.** Medium, scale, quantity, technique, materials, process. Concrete nouns. "I will explore themes of identity" is a non-statement; "I will produce a series of eight 2×3 m woven panels using discarded fishing net collected in Gaspésie" is a project.
2. **Why it matters — the artistic question.** What is genuinely unresolved for you? Research and exploration grants (CALQ *Exploration and Research*) exist precisely to **fund risk**; the program's stated purpose is to support "the risk inherent in the research process." So do not pretend to certainty you don't have. Name the uncertainty and make it rigorous. But: *uncertain about the outcome* ≠ *vague about the method*. The method must be airtight.
3. **Why now, and why this form.** What in the practice's trajectory makes this the necessary next move?
4. **Where, with whom, and what's confirmed.** Named venues, named collaborators, named residencies, with their status (confirmed / invited / in discussion). Name real people and institutions. Unnamed partners read as fiction.
5. **What happens to the work afterward.** Exhibition, publication, dissemination, integration into practice. Feasibility and impact both live here.
6. **Timeline.** Phases with dates. See below.

**Paraphrase the question back in your answer.** If the form asks "How will this project contribute to the development of your practice?", the answer should open with something like "This project develops my practice by…". The assessor is scanning for the answer to *that* question, and it is one of the cheapest points available.

## Artist statement / description of practice

150–300 words is the working range for most funders. Distinct from the project description: it is about the **practice**, not the proposal, and it exists to make the proposal legible as a *next step* rather than a detour.

Cover: what you make, why you make it, what questions drive the work, what materials/processes recur, and where this project sits in that arc.

Do **not**: recycle the same statement across every application unedited. Assessors read a lot of applications; a statement written for a different context is visible.

If the artist's positioning is genuinely part of the work — language, cultural background, geography, disability, official-language minority status, diasporic experience — **state it plainly and specifically** rather than gesturing. Vagueness here reads as either boilerplate or discomfort. But it must be *true and load-bearing for the work*, not a strategic garnish.

## Work plan / timeline

Funders want an action plan that is **reasonable, realistic, phased, dated, and aware of when the money actually arrives.**

Give phases, not a blur:

| Phase | Dates | Activity | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Research | Sept–Nov 2026 | Site visits, archival work, material tests | Test panels, working notes |
| Production | Dec 2026–Feb 2027 | Fabrication of 8 panels | Completed series |
| Presentation | Mar 2027 | Install + solo exhibition, [venue] | Public exhibition, documentation |

Two traps:
- **Starting before you're funded.** Most funders will not fund activities that begin before the application is submitted/approved. CALQ requires submission at least 4 weeks before the activity starts; CCA's Artistic Creation requires applying any time *before the start date*. A timeline whose first phase begins last month is an eligibility failure.
- **A timeline that doesn't match the budget.** If the budget has three months of subsistence and the timeline is six months long, the application has just told the jury it can't count.

## Support material — often the first thing they look at

For most visual-arts juries the images *are* the artistic-merit score. Text supports the images; it does not substitute for them.

**You get about ten minutes.** The Canada Council instructs assessors to view as much support material as they need — "generally, this means **10 minutes maximum per application**." That is the budget for your *entire* package. Extra material does not buy extra minutes; it spends the same ten on weaker work. Front-load the strongest image, the strongest 30 seconds. Curate toward the proposal, not toward a career.

- **Follow the spec exactly** — number of images, file formats, video duration, page counts. Over-delivering is not generosity; it is an instruction-following failure, and excess material may simply be ignored or the application penalized.
- **Professional-quality documentation.** Not phone snapshots of works on a studio floor. Include installation views — juries need to see how the work occupies space.
- **Image list** with title, medium, dimensions, year for every image. This is mandatory nearly everywhere and routinely botched.
- **Curate for the proposal.** Show the work that makes the proposed project look inevitable. Recent work generally beats greatest hits.
- **Video:** put the strongest 30 seconds first. Assessors do not watch to the end.
- **Letters of support:** ask a month ahead; ask people with standing in the field; ask whether they can write a *strong* letter; get them on letterhead. For travel/circulation grants, an **invitation letter or venue contract is usually mandatory** — its absence is an eligibility screen-out, not a lost point.

## Sentence-level rules

- Clear, concise, compelling. Active voice. Short sentences. Concrete nouns.
- Confident and enthusiastic, without sentimentality or salesmanship.
- No jargon, no theory-speak, no "obscurantist, ostentatious verbosity." The jury is expert but cross-disciplinary; a curator of textiles may be scoring a video artist.
- Do not write "see attached." Answer in the box.
- Respect word limits. Under is fine. Over is not.
- Read it aloud. Then have **two** people who are not you read it — ideally one peer in the discipline, one outside it entirely. Put their comments into action rather than defending the draft.

## Anti-patterns that cost real points

| Pattern | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| "Explores themes of memory, identity and belonging" | Says nothing. Appears in a third of all applications. Never fundable on its own. |
| Abstract project, no medium/scale/quantity | Assessor cannot picture the work; feasibility unscoreable. |
| Round-number budget ($10,000 = "materials") | Reads as invented. Kills the feasibility/administrative score. |
| No artist fee, or a token one | Undervalues labour; CALQ explicitly assesses fairness of compensation. |
| Mandate-cosplay ("this project advances equity and reconciliation and digital innovation and…") | Juries are working artists. They can tell. Pick what's true. |
| Unnamed partners ("a gallery in Seoul") | Reads as aspiration, not plan. Name it or drop the claim. |
| Deficit budget | Signals the project cannot actually happen. |
| Generic recycled artist statement | Visible, and signals the artist didn't read the program. |
| Passive, hedging voice ("it is hoped that the work might…") | Reads as no conviction. |
| Applying to the wrong component | Fatal. No amount of writing quality survives it. |

## The jury simulation (run this before shipping)

Adopt the voice of a peer assessor on their 60th application of the day. Then:

1. **Score each criterion out of its published weight.** Not vibes — numbers, with a one-line justification each.
2. **Check each minimum threshold.** A category below threshold sinks the application regardless of the total.
3. **Name the single weakest criterion** and fix that one first. Improving an already-strong criterion is wasted effort.
4. **The repeatability test:** state the project in one sentence, from memory, without looking at the draft. If you can't, the jury can't advocate for it in the room.
5. **The stranger test:** would someone outside the discipline understand what is being made?
6. **The "so what" pass:** for each paragraph, ask what it earns. If it earns nothing, cut it and spend the words on the criterion with the biggest weight.

Report the simulated scores to the artist honestly, including when the answer is "this is not competitive yet." A flattering review is worthless — the real jury will not be kind, and the artist has a limited number of applications per year (CALQ: 3 in flight, 5 per year) to spend.
