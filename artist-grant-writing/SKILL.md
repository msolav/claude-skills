# Artist Grant Writing (Quebec / Canada)

Grant writing for artists is not persuasive-essay writing. It is **writing for a peer jury under time pressure, against published criteria, with a weighted score.** Everything in this skill flows from that fact.

## The one rule that governs everything

**Never invent a program's rules.** Program names, deadlines, maximum amounts, criteria weightings, and eligible expenses change every year — and Canada Council restructured its entire grant architecture in 2025. Anything in this skill's reference files is a *starting map*, not a source of truth.

Before drafting a single sentence, **fetch the live guidelines for the exact component being applied to** and write the criteria + weightings at the top of the working draft. If you cannot access the guidelines (many CCA guidelines live behind the applicant portal), say so and ask the artist to paste them in. Do not proceed on assumption.

## Workflow

### 1. Scope the request before writing
Establish, and state back to the artist:
- **Funder + exact component** (e.g. CALQ *Travel*, not "a CALQ grant")
- **Deadline and decision timeline** — CALQ is largely rolling with a 4-week minimum lead and ~8-week response; CCA components have fixed dates
- **The activity verb**: research / create / produce / experiment / travel / exhibit / tour / publish / develop
- **Criteria and their weights**, verbatim from the guidelines
- **Amount requested** and what other money exists

If the artist has picked the wrong component (common — travel money sought inside a creation grant, artist fees claimed inside a travel grant), say so immediately. See `references/funders.md`.

### 2. Interview before you draft
Do not write from a one-line prompt. You need raw material or the text will be generic — and generic is the single most reliable way to lose. Ask for: the actual work (images, links), what the artist is genuinely uncertain about, why *now*, why *this* place/partner, who has confirmed what, and what happens to the work afterward.

Prefer the artist's own words. Your job is structure, precision, and pressure-testing — not voice replacement. See `references/ai-and-integrity.md`; the Council explicitly warns that over-reliance on AI dilutes artistic voice, and applicants are fully accountable for their content.

### 3. Draft against the scoring grid, section by section
Map every paragraph to a criterion. If a paragraph scores nothing, cut it. See `references/writing-craft.md` for section-by-section craft, sentence-level rules, and the anti-pattern list.

Order of construction that works:
1. **Project description** (the spine — what, why, how, when, where, with whom)
2. **Work plan / timeline** (proves feasibility)
3. **Budget** (proves feasibility harder; see `references/budgets-and-fees.md`)
4. **Artist statement / practice** (situates the project in a trajectory)
5. **Support material list** (the jury often looks at this *first*)
6. **Summary line** — write last, from the finished thing

### 4. Run the jury simulation
Before declaring a draft done, review it in the voice of a tired peer assessor who has read 60 applications today, using only the published criteria. Score each criterion out of its weight. Name the weakest criterion and fix that one first. Assessors rank comparatively against other applications in the same cohort — "good" is not the bar; "better than the next one" is.

### 5. Ship checklist
- Every question answered *in the box provided* — never "see attached"
- Word/character limits respected (under is fine; over is disqualifying)
- Budget arithmetic verified; expenses and revenues balance
- Support material labelled, correctly formatted, within the stated count/duration
- Confirmations attached (invitation letter, venue contract, residency acceptance) — for travel and circulation grants these are usually *mandatory*, and their absence is a screening failure, not a scoring one
- Submitted before the deadline, not at it

## Non-negotiables

**Follow the instructions.** The most consistent piece of advice from jurors and program officers across every source is banal and true: strong artists lose to weaker artists because they filled the form out wrong. A clean, correctly completed application is itself evidence that the artist can execute a project.

**Feasibility is a scored criterion, not paperwork.** A vague timeline and a round-number budget cost real points.

**Pay yourself.** Undervaluing artist labour reads as amateurism, and CALQ explicitly assesses whether remuneration is "fair and equitable, reflecting the talent, skills and number of hours worked." For visual artists, anchor fees to the CARFAC-RAAV Minimum Recommended Fee Schedule.

**Do not pander.** Align genuinely with a funder's mandate or apply elsewhere. Juries are working artists; they can smell mandate-cosplay, and reverse-engineering a practice to match a priority reads as dishonest.

**Rejection is normal.** Many programs fund 20–30% of applicants. Request the score breakdown and written feedback (CCA provides both), then reapply. Persistence across cycles is a strategy, not a consolation.

## Scope honesty

Funders do not publish successful applications; the Council releases funding *results* (who got what), not application texts. This skill therefore derives "what wins" from published assessment criteria, funder decision-making documentation, juror-facing guides, and arts-service-organization workshops — not from leaked winning applications. Be equally honest with the artist: no one can promise a formula.
